CONTEXT
=====================================================================

Eugene Wei's "Status as a Service" (StaaS) essay argues that
social platforms are fundamentally status games. Users join to
accumulate social capital. The platforms that win are the ones
that design the best proof-of-work mechanisms — tasks that are
hard enough to confer status on those who complete them, but
accessible enough to attract new participants.

Wei's key concepts:

  Proof of Work
    Every social network needs a mechanism that makes status
    feel earned. Twitter has wit. TikTok has dancing. Instagram
    has aesthetics. The proof of work requirement filters who
    can accumulate status and how fast.

  Social Capital Utility Function
    Users implicitly ask: can I accumulate social capital here?
    If the answer is no — or if existing users have too much
    head start — new users don't join.

  Status Games Are Invisible
    The most successful platforms don't look like status games
    from the outside. They look like creativity tools, or
    communication tools, or productivity tools. The status
    game runs underneath.

  the methodology, the canon terms — understanding them
  signals genuine engagement. That's proof of work. That's
  status. The friction is not a bug, it's the feature. High
  context creates a defensible community. You don't want
  drive-by participants. You want people who did the work.

  POSITION B (Friction as Barrier):
  Every unnecessary complexity is a door you didn't open.
  The cathedral underneath should be accessible. The humble
  surface exists precisely so the cathedral doesn't intimidate.
  If the proof of work is too high, the community never forms.
  Simplicity is not compromise — it's strategy.
